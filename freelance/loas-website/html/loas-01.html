Drunk, Driving, and Dangerous

3 D's of the city vientaine

02

It's a weekday evening in the Laos capital of Vientiane, and the first of dozens of calls comes in for volunteer-led emergency response team Vientiane Rescue. First responders are at the scene within minutes and immediately check on two young drivers who lost control of their motorbikes and crashed into a ditch.

Also at the scene is Sebastian Perret, a French paramedic and former firefighter, who with seven local volunteers started Vientiane Rescue in 2010. It remains the country’s only free, emergency responder service.

With the rising affluence in Laos, the country’s roads have seen an increase in traffic, resulting in more accidents. Most of the crashes are attributed to speeding and drunk driving. Before Vientiane Rescue, Sebastien says that victims of serious accidents would often be left to die on the street. To date, Vientiane Rescue has saved more than 60,000 people - from just 300 in 2010 to over 15,000 in 2019.

03

Across town, flashing, neon lights illuminate a different kind of nightlife - the darkened stage of a packed dance club. Venues like this draw a boisterous crowd looking for a good time. Alcohol lubricates the evening for many young Laotians in their 20s and 30s. According to the World Health Organisation, Laos is the third biggest consumer of alcohol in Southeast Asia, with 7.5 litres consumed per person per year.

04

Outside this club, a drunk, young man recovers on a sofa. According to official sources, underage drinking in Laos is common in restaurants and bars. Barriers to access are minimal with alcohol being affordable and easily available. In a move to promote health and safety among its residents, Vientiane’s Mayor has banned the sale of alcohol or alcoholic beverages before 4pm in the capital.

05

The ban might prevent people from drinking earlier in the day, but is unlikely to affect alcohol sales, as revellers usually consume alcohol after work. Before the night is up, tables at F&B outlets like this are littered with empty bottles.

06
Motorcycles are the most popular form of transport in Vientiane.
Many riders are underaged or do not have a proper driving licence. They also tend to drive under the influence of alcohol, hence drunk driving is the leading cause of road fatalities in Laos.
07

Vientiane Rescue’s ambulance are dispatched from four stations located near ‘hot spots’ across the city. Braving crowded road conditions, volunteer drivers weave in and out of busy traffic to get to a crash site within three to seven minutes. On a busy night, Vientiane Rescue’s trained first-aid and EMS volunteers respond to an average of 20 calls. The service is supported by private donations, and operates on a tight budget of US$5,000 a month.

08
Accidents usually involve motorcyclists, and since helmets are seldom worn, the risk of getting seriously hurt is greater.
Volunteers attend to varying severities of injuries; from minor cuts and scrapes to broken limbs and horrific accidents that end in death - as many as 50 a month.
09

Vientiane Rescue has touched the lives of people from all walks of life. Through the years, volunteers have earned the trust and respect of the community. The free emergency service is often the first and only place people turn to in an emergency. Besides road accidents, Vientiane Rescue’s teams also fight fires and deliver babies. This evening, volunteers respond to a domestic violence case, which would also involve local police.

10
Volunteers

who made it all possible

Even though road accidents are common in Vientiane, scenes of wreckage and tragedy still draw bystanders. But for some who have witnessed one too many vehicular mishaps, it was no longer enough to simply stand by and do nothing. They were instead moved to make a difference, signing up as volunteers with Vientiane Rescue.

Sebastien says the qualities they look for are courage, solidarity, compassion, altruism, commitment and sacrifice. Uniforms are only given out after trainees show up regularly for at least three months.

12

Ton was so inspired by his father's passion that he asked to become a volunteer at 11 years old.

He now volunteers two to three times a week, sometimes without his dad. Father and son have dinner with the rest of the family, before heading out for their shift at Dong Dok station.

13

In order to continue with Vientiane Rescue, Ton has proven to his parents that he can juggle school work and volunteering. His commitment is clear, becoming one of the youngest volunteers to complete EMT training. Ai says he is proud to see his son mature over the years, and believes that first aid rescue has taught him the importance of responsible driving.

The number of badges on Ai's uniform is testament to his achievements over the years. He is now in charge of the diving, fire and rope rescue teams. His interest in first aid and rescue was nurtured since young. And he continues to develop his skills by continually training.

14

All's quiet for now at Don Dok station, where Kik, Johnson, Ai and Ton volunteer. Don Dok is the largest of four stations run by Vientiane Rescue. They also have one station in the southern city of Pakse, and two in the northern part of the country in Vang Vieng and Xiengkhuang.

15

Ai and Ton check the pressure of the station's supply of oxygen tanks to make sure they function properly. When the dam collapsed in southeast Laos in 2018, Ai took leave from his job to represent Vientiane Rescue during the search and rescue operation.

He says there were moments he feared for the lives of his team of volunteers, as they had to search for survivors in the river at near zero visibility. There was also the lurking threat of UXOs spread across the area by the floods. But Ai understood that risking their lives was integral to what they signed up for as first responders.

16

happy birthday!

Johnson joins Ai and Ton at Don Dok station, and starts his shift on a happy note. He surprises call centre manager Mimi with a birthday cake. The fun ends abruptly when the phones start ringing, and Mimi returns to work.

17
On the Road

where all the hustle happens

Johnson is sent out on his first emergency of the night. He replaces the cake in his hand with the radio receiver in the ambulance, which receives updates from Mimi at Don Dok station, as the team heads out on their rescue mission.

18

Basic Controls

Traffic Warnings

At the wheel next to Johnson, Ai skillfully manoeuvres through rush-hour traffic with speed and confidence. It can be risky and harrowing when other drivers on the road break the law, for example, by speeding or skipping red lights.

19

Thankfully

The accident isn’t serious, and the team quickly transfers the shaken victim, a motorcyclist, onto the stretcher. Back in the ambulance, it is a family affair with Ai driving the team to the nearest hospital and Ton in the back chatting and watching over the patient.

20

Back at Don Dok station

Kik has just arrived for her shift. Trained as an emergency medical technician (EMT), she is in charge of briefing and training the newcomers in first aid.

21

But Kik’s quiet night ends soon enough when an emergency call comes in. She hops into the ambulance with her team and preps them from the front seat.

22

A self-confessed risk-taker, Kik is in her element in the thick of the action. Her steely concentration is nothing less than admirable. She directs her team of volunteers to move the victim onto a stretcher after wrapping a brace around his neck. The young driver is semi-conscious and visibly drunk after crashing his motorbike into the side of the road.

23

Following administrative processes to the letter is key to ensuring all accident victims Vientiane Rescue delivers to the hospital are given the best chances of surviving their injuries. For this volunteer, it means referring to the scribbled notes on her hand when filling in a detailed report about the accident.

24

At the hospital, Sebastien chats with volunteers after dropping off an accident survivor at A&E. Having lived in the country for nearly a decade, his command of Lao is fairly strong and has developed a relaxed and jovial relationship with the volunteers. Their camaraderie is evident. Over the years, Sebastien and his teams have also developed a respectful relationship with staff at the local hospitals, like Friendship Hospital. This has helped to improve the survival rate of accident victims, which has improved morale among volunteers.

25
Winding Down

the story of the rise of Vientaine rescue

After another hectic night, it's time to fill up on piping hot bowls of noodle soup. Sebastien takes his volunteers out for supper at one of the popular street side stalls. He cares deeply for his volunteers and worries about their future, particularly for those who volunteer full-time with Vientiane Rescue.

Sebastien wants an equitable life for them. This means finding more avenues of funding so he can pay full-timers a living wage.
26

Back at the station, the rest of the volunteers return from their rescue missions, and catch up over a home-cooked dinner. The atmosphere is light and filled with laughter, showing a deep bond between them strengthened by the sheer amount of time they spend together. Their friendships help to soften the often bloody scenes they see every day. Sebastien says that when the people they save and their families donate items to the stations to show their gratitude, the thoughtfulness raises the spirits of volunteers and gives them the impetus to continue to save lives.

27

Some of these friendships have blossomed into romances. Exhausted from a long day at the office, Kik decides to have an early night. Before heading to bed, she video calls her fiance, who is also a volunteer with Vientiane Rescue. He was part of the 2018 cave rescue team in Chiang Rai, Thailand. Kik says he was coaching and advising her when she first joined. Their shared experiences and interest in helping people has brought them closer and made it easier for their relationship to succeed.

28

Vientiane Rescue volunteers each have their own private space in the dorms at each station. But those still on shift find any chance they can get to take a quick nap during lull periods. This helps them stay alert after a long day when an emergency comes in the dead of the night.

29

A new day rolls in at Don Dok station, and it is Mimi’s turn to surprise another volunteer with a birthday cake. The burning candles light up the smiling faces in the darkened room. Within the huddle of this touching moment, the future of the Vientiane Rescue family looks bright.
